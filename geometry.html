Geometry of Meaning

How Buchenberg measures translation quality using vector mathematics — illustrated with real sentences from The Hound of the Baskervilles.

Scatter plot uses pre-calculated embeddings (multilingual-e5-large, 1024 dimensions) from static JSON. The Measure similarity section loads Xenova/multilingual-e5-large via Transformers.js (~100 MB on first load, then cached by the browser). This may take a moment on slow connections.

The mathematics behind the pipeline

01

A sentence becomes a point

The embedding model reads a sentence and outputs a list of 1024 numbers. Each number captures a different aspect of meaning — grammar, topic, tone, context.

Two sentences that mean the same thing — even in different languages — produce similar lists of numbers. This is what makes cross-lingual comparison possible.

"Holmes sat at the table."
→ [0.021, −0.147, 0.382, 0.009, −0.251, … × 1024]
02

1024 dimensions of meaning

We perceive space in 3 dimensions — length, width, height. A colour is a point in 3D space: (R, G, B). A sentence lives in 1024-dimensional space.

We cannot visualise 1024 dimensions directly, so we use projection techniques (like UMAP below) to collapse them to 2D — preserving the most important neighbourhood relationships.

Sentences that are semantically close end up near each other in that 2D projection, even though the full 1024D geometry is much richer.

03

Cosine similarity — measuring the angle

Given two vectors A and B, cosine similarity measures the angle between them — not the distance. This matters: a long sentence and a short sentence saying the same thing should score 1.0, not be penalised for length.

cos(θ) = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)

1.0 → identical meaning
0.5 → loosely related
0.0 → unrelated

Buchenberg computes two cosine scores per translation: the translation score (EN vs. translated) and the back score (EN vs. back-translated). Both feed the final winner formula.

04

The winner formula

Five models compete for each sentence. The winner is selected by a weighted score that combines geometric similarity with a blind LLM judge (Gemma4:31b).

composite = (back_score + translation_score) / 2

final = 0.4 × composite
+ 0.6 × judge_avg

judge_avg = (grammar + naturalness
+ fidelity) / 3

The judge carries 60% of the weight — linguistic quality matters more than raw cosine proximity.

Two readings of this space

The geometry above is not just mathematics. A writer and a philosopher described this space long before anyone could compute it.

Borges · 1941

The Library and the Compass

In The Library of Babel, Jorge Luis Borges imagined a library containing every possible book — every combination of letters, in every language. Its librarians spend their lives searching for one meaningful page, and die without finding it. The library contains everything, and is therefore worth nothing: information without selection is noise.

The embedding space above is that library, made continuous. Every possible sentence in every language has coordinates here, and almost all of them are gibberish. What makes the space usable is not its content but its index — a function that can tell meaning from noise.

That is what Buchenberg builds. Five models generate candidates near the point where the meaning of the original lives; cosine similarity and a blind judge measure which candidate landed closest. The librarian finally has a compass. It is also why the judge carries 60% of the final score: the scoring function is a more valuable artifact than any single translation it selects.

Borges made the point twice. In Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote (1939), a modern writer recreates Cervantes word for word — and the identical text means something entirely different. Form does not carry meaning; context does. Menard is the problem of translation, stated precisely, fourteen years before philosophy caught up.

Wittgenstein · 1953

Meaning as Use

The young Wittgenstein of the Tractatus (1921) believed meaning was logical form: a sentence mirrors reality, and translation preserves the mirror. The late Wittgenstein of the Philosophical Investigations (1953) corrected himself: the meaning of a word is its use in the language. Concepts are held together not by a common essence but by family resemblance — overlapping similarities, like the faces of one family.

The scatter plot above is family resemblance, quantified. The embedding model learns meaning from nothing but use — the company each word keeps across billions of sentences. Cosine similarity is the degree of resemblance between two members of the family; the UMAP clusters you can see are families of meaning, drawn by no one.

The whole pipeline follows the late Wittgenstein. Back-translation tests whether meaning survives the round trip, not whether form does. And the judge’s naturalness score measures exactly what he called competence in a language game: does this sentence live comfortably in the life of the target language?

Edwin Abbott’s Flatland — book 21 in this corpus — completes the trio: Abbott supplies the geometry, Borges the semantics, Wittgenstein the bridge between them. Buchenberg translates literature with a technology whose problems were stated most precisely by literature itself.
