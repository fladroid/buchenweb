Kandinsky · 1911

On the Spiritual in Art

On 2 January 1911, Wassily Kandinsky attended a Schoenberg concert in Munich. What he heard pushed him toward abstraction: within weeks he painted Impression III (Konzert), and within the year he published On the Spiritual in Art.

Its central claim: color, form, and sound are not things that illustrate one another. They are different projections of the same inner content. A yellow is not like a trumpet — both are surfaces of one underlying vibration, reached through different organs.

Kandinsky had no mathematics for this intuition. He could only paint it. The claim that content exists before the sense that perceives it had to wait a century for an unexpected proof.

Scriabin · 1910

Prometheus: The Poem of Fire

Alexander Scriabin went further than theory — he changed the score. Prometheus includes a part for the clavier à lumières, a keyboard that plays light: each key floods the hall with a color.

The light part is not decoration. It is written into the score like the strings or the brass — which means the work is incomplete when performed in only one channel. Hearing Prometheus without seeing it is, by the composer’s own definition, hearing half the piece.

Scriabin’s premise is operational, not mystical: if the content is one, a single sense under-samples it. Add a channel — recover more of the original.

Buchenberg · 2026

The embedding is already synesthesia

When multilingual-e5-large reads a sentence, it projects it into a point in 1024-dimensional space. That point is not text. It is not an image, and it is not a sound. It is pure content with no sense attached — the mathematical realization of Kandinsky’s intuition about the common layer beneath the modalities.

And if the point precedes the sense, then the choice of channel is arbitrary. The UMAP scatter on the Geometry page, a melody, a generative fingerprint — these are equal projections of the same object. Shadows of the same sphere.

Which closes the circle with Abbott. The Square in Flatland could only ever see one projection — the shadow on his plane — and from it he could never reconstruct the sphere. But a Flatlander given two projections, shadow and sound, recovers more of the object than either channel carries alone. Sonification is the extra dimension of observation that Abbott’s Square was never granted.

One chronological footnote: Kandinsky and Scriabin precede the Tractatus (1921). The artists arrived first — again. As Borges preceded Wittgenstein, as Abbott preceded the theory of reference frames.

This page adds no new data. Every note you will hear and every shape you will see already exists in the database — as a number. We have only changed the sense through which you look. If a dissonance grates on your ear at sentence 847, you have just heard a low finalni_score. You did not read the data point. You felt it. — the X-Ray attitude, taken to its conclusion: not only looking inside, but choosing the organ you look with.

The Tapestry

A book as woven fabric. Each cell is one sentence; reading order runs left to right, top to bottom. Color is the winner’s final score — or the winning model, if you switch the channel. Gray cells are not yet woven. Hover for the sentence; click to open the book in the Reader.

Absolute maps the final score onto a fixed 0.60–1.00 scale — the honest picture. It is mostly green because the pipeline is consistently good: half of all sentences score above 0.966. Relative colors each sentence by its percentile rank within this book, stretching the narrow band of real differences across the full gradient: a red cell is not a bad translation, only one of the weakest in this fabric. Absolute shows how good the weave is; Relative shows where it is thinnest.

The Sound of Translation

The same fabric, heard instead of seen. Each sentence becomes a note in reading order; its final score sets the pitch — the stronger the weave, the higher it sings. Notes are locked to a pentatonic scale, so any book plays in tune. A sentence not yet woven is a rest: you hear its silence.

Relative maps each sentence to its percentile rank within the book, spending the full range of pitches; Absolute ties pitch to the raw 0.60–1.00 score, so a consistently strong book sings mostly high. This is the third shadow of the same sphere — beside the scatter of Geometry and the cloth of The Tapestry.

Sentence Fingerprints

Every sentence has a shape. Its embedding vector — 1,024 numbers that capture its meaning — becomes a radial fingerprint: 64 sectors, each holding a slice of the vector; its length is the slice’s magnitude, its colour the slice’s mean. A faithful translation should produce a fingerprint that looks like the original. No reference translation needed — the shape speaks for itself.

The fourth shadow of the same sphere — beside the scatter of Geometry, the cloth of The Tapestry, and the melody of The Sound of Translation.
