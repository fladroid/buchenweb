What We Don't Measure

I. What the measuring apparatus cannot see

1. The cosine metric is blind to text that was never translated

Our embedder is deliberately multilingual: it is built so that "cat", "Katze", "gatto" and "mačka" land close together in vector space. That is exactly what makes it useful for comparing meaning across languages — and exactly why it cannot notice when no translation happened at all. A French exclamation left untouched inside a German translation scores 0.99. Measured across the corpus: 2,784 such cases (0.17%). The only defence is the LLM judge, which catches them reliably and scores them zero.

2. The cosine metric is blind to broken script

Serbian translations containing Latin letters stranded inside Cyrillic text — the result of transliteration rules that do not cover letters absent from the Serbian Latin alphabet — receive a cosine score of 0.95. The proper name is still recognisable to the embedder, so the damage is invisible to it. 1,466 translations, 0.92% of the Serbian corpus.

3. The cosine metric is blind to broken grammar with intact keywords

A Croatian sentence in which the verb had been replaced by an unrelated one, producing nonsense, scored 0.97. If the content words survive, the metric is satisfied.

4. The weights in our formula do not describe their real influence

The winner is chosen by 0.4 × cosine + 0.6 × judge. But a weight only matters in proportion to how much the component varies. Across the corpus the judge's scores spread with a standard deviation of 0.207; the cosine component spreads by 0.028 — seven and a half times narrower. The practical consequence is that the ranking of candidates is determined roughly 8% by cosine and 92% by the judge, not 40/60.

We have chosen not to "correct" this. Rescaling the components so that cosine truly carried 40% of the vote would systematically promote the failures described in points 1 to 3 — the cases where cosine is confidently wrong. The mismatch between stated and actual weight is currently protecting us.

II. What we systematically penalise, and probably should not

5. Deliberate departure from linguistic norm

The judge measures fluency. Literature breaks fluency on purpose.

From our own corpus — Edwin Abbott's Flatland, translated into German. The original reads:

a SOMETHING-WHICH-YOU-DO-NOT-AS-YET-KNOW-A-NAME-FOR-BUT-WHICH-WE-CALL-A-CUBE

This is not clumsy writing. It is a deliberate typographic device: a word-monster built to imitate the bewilderment of a two-dimensional being confronted with a third dimension. The first translation preserved it faithfully. The self-refinement step erased it and produced a smooth, idiomatic German sentence instead.

Our score rewarded that erasure with +0.157 — the largest single improvement in the entire sample. The system identified the removal of the author's device as the best correction it made that day.

The same tendency is visible in aggregate: of the judge's three axes, naturalness rises fastest through refinement (+0.061 to +0.078), ahead of both grammar and fidelity. On the overwhelming majority of sentences this is genuine improvement. On the small fraction where an author was being deliberately difficult, it is silent damage.

III. What does not exist in our world at all

6. Everything above the sentence

Our unit of processing is the sentence. Whatever was arrangement — line breaks, stanzas, visual shape, tables, captions, typographic play — disappears when the text is parsed and never reaches any measurement. This is not a bad score; it is an absence.

The clearest illustration is not from our corpus but from a familiar critique of machine translation: a classical Chinese poem of twenty characters, arranged as five lines of four. The arrangement is part of the poem. No machine translation reproduces that layer — ours included — and not because it evaluates it poorly. It never sees it.

This is not hypothetical for us. Our library contains a verse drama, processed as a sequence of prose sentences. It contains a book built around geometric diagrams that do not survive into the translation at all. A book carrying substantial illustration would fare no better: our pipeline has no concept of a page.

7. Context

Every sentence is translated in isolation. Who is speaking to whom, what register the relationship demands, what happened in the previous paragraph — none of it enters. We tested two ways of supplying that context and abandoned both: the effect on the translation was smaller than the variation between two identical calls to the same model. The signal was beneath the noise.

IV. What we know is incomplete

  • Coverage gaps exist — combinations of book, language and configuration where translation was started but not finished. Known, catalogued, and deliberately left alone while a winner exists for those sentences.
  • Two generations of models are mixed in the corpus history. Models retired in 2026 produced a large share of the existing translations, alongside their replacements — the two show a noticeably different stylistic signature in places, though the cause has not been isolated. Aggregate statistics that ignore this distinction can mislead.
  • The judge is both a player and the measuring instrument. Any preference we read out of win statistics is a preference of our evaluator, not a truth about language. We have no independent measure against which to check it.
Why this page exists

Every measurement system has a shape, and that shape decides what it can see. This page documents what ours cannot — the blind spots we have found, the things we penalise that we probably should not, and the entire layers of a text that never reach our measurements at all.

None of this is hidden in a footnote. A pipeline that publishes only its successes is a black box with good manners. The findings below come from our own audit of 1.6 million translations, and several of them overturned what we believed when we started.

This page is in English only — a deliberate exception to the site's five-language interface, made because the content is technical and still growing.

The honest formulation

We do not measure faithfulness to the author. We measure faithfulness to the norms of the target language. Most of the time these coincide. When they do not, we cannot tell.

A note on what this page is not

This is not a list of bugs awaiting repair. Most of these are properties of the architecture we chose, and some of them cannot be fixed without building a different project. Naming a limit is not the same as apologising for it — knowing what you cannot see, and treating that as information rather than defeat, is the whole point.
